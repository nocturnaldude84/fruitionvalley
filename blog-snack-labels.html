How to Read Snack Labels

Most packaging is designed to catch your eye on the front. The honest version of the story is always on the back. Here is how to read it in under a minute.

By Fruition Valley June 2026 8 min read

Most parents standing in a supermarket aisle with a child pulling at their sleeve don't have thirty minutes to audit an ingredient list. You flip the pack over, scan it, spot a word you can't pronounce, and either put it back or just put it in the trolley. This guide is for parents who want to move on a little more confidently. You don't need a nutrition degree. You just need to know which parts of a label actually tell you something useful, and which parts are mostly packaging noise.

The snack aisle is designed to work against you. Front-of-pack claims, character illustrations, and health-adjacent words like "wholesome," "natural," and "goodness" are there to create an impression before you get to the ingredient list. The ingredient list is the part that matters. Everything that follows in this guide works backwards from that one starting point.

Start with the serving size, not the big numbers

Before you look at any figures on the nutrition panel, find the serving size. It is usually listed at the top of the table, and it catches out a lot of parents. A pack that looks like a single portion for a child might say "serves 2" or list all its values per 100g rather than per pack.

If the figures are per 100g, you need to adjust. A 35g snack pouch contains roughly 35 percent of whatever is printed per 100g. That sounds obvious once you read it, but most people skip this step entirely and treat the nutrition table as if it applies to the whole thing. Often it does not.

Get the serving size right first. The sugar number, the sodium number, the calorie count: all of them are meaningless without knowing what portion they refer to. Once you have adjusted for the actual portion your child will eat, the rest of the table becomes useful.

Sugar: the figure that matters most

There is no shortage of opinion on children and sugar, but the practical guidance is fairly clear. Most paediatric nutrition guidelines, including those from the WHO and NHS, suggest limiting free sugars to under 19g per day for children aged 4 to 6, and 24g per day for ages 7 to 10. Free sugars means anything added during manufacturing, plus sugars naturally present in honey, syrups, and fruit juices. It does not include the sugars naturally present in whole fruit.

On Indian packaging under FSSAI format, you will see "total carbohydrates" broken down into "of which sugars." That second figure is what you want. A fruit-based snack will show sugar here because dried fruit contains natural fructose. That is not the same as added sugar, and it does not behave the same way in the body because the fiber in the fruit slows its absorption. The way to tell natural and added sugars apart is not through the nutrition table but through the ingredient list.

A label showing 12g of sugar per serving could mean very different things depending on whether that 12g comes from dried mango or from glucose syrup and sucrose. The nutrition panel cannot tell you which. The ingredient list can.

The ingredient list is the honest part of the label

Ingredients are listed in descending order by weight. The first ingredient is the most abundant thing in the product. If sugar, glucose syrup, or any sweetener variant appears in the first three positions, you are looking at a product built around added sweetness, with other ingredients filling in around it. The fruit imagery on the front, the vitamin claim, the color of the packaging: none of it changes what is actually inside.

Manufacturers are aware that parents scan for the word "sugar," so added sugar appears under many different names on ingredient lists. A few to know: glucose, dextrose, maltose, corn syrup, fructose syrup, invert sugar, sucrose. These are all added sugars under different names. The word "sugar" is just one of them, and it is not always the one that appears.

If the first ingredient is a recognizable whole food, an actual fruit, a grain, a dairy ingredient, a legume, a nut or seed, that is a meaningfully better starting point than a product that leads with a sugar or refined starch. It does not make the product automatically good, but it tells you what the product is fundamentally made of. That matters when you are packing something five days a week.

Four categories of additives worth knowing about

Most parents know to look for "artificial colours" but fewer know which ones to watch for, or why. Here are the four categories that appear most often in packaged children's snacks.

Artificial colours. In the EU, products containing six specific azo dyes must carry a warning that they may have an adverse effect on activity and attention in children. These include tartrazine (E102), sunset yellow (E110), quinoline yellow (E104), carmoisine (E122), allura red (E129), and ponceau 4R (E124). India's FSSAI has provisions in this area too, though enforcement varies. If you see E-numbers in the E100 to E180 range on a children's snack, it is worth a quick check. The precautionary argument is simple: a snack made from real food does not need synthetic colors.

Preservatives. Sodium benzoate (E211) is one to know by name. Research has associated it with increased hyperactivity in children, particularly when combined with certain synthetic dyes. Most dried fruit snacks, and most snacks made primarily from real food, do not need it at all.

Artificial flavours. The phrase "natural and artificial flavours" tells you almost nothing specific. "Natural flavour" is a regulated but broadly defined term that can legally be derived from sources most parents would not expect. Products made from genuinely simple ingredients list the actual ingredient, not a flavour category. If the label says "mango flavour" rather than "dried mango," that is worth noticing.

Emulsifiers and stabilisers. These appear more often in processed dairy-based snacks and cereal bars than in simple dried fruit products. They are used to maintain texture and extend shelf life. Their presence is not always cause for concern, but it signals a more heavily processed product than one whose ingredient list reads like a shopping list.

What "natural" actually means on a food label

In most markets, including India, "natural" is not a legally regulated term on food packaging. There is no standard that prevents a product calling itself natural while containing added sugars, preservatives, and synthetic dyes. It is a marketing description that can mean anything the manufacturer chooses it to mean. The same applies to "wholesome," "goodness," "pure," and similar front-of-pack language.

The only reliable check is the ingredient list. A genuinely natural product reads like a short shopping list: dried mango. Or: oats, dates, almonds. Every ingredient is something you could buy separately. There is nothing that requires a chemical dictionary to identify.

If a product has fifteen ingredients including three E-numbers and two flavour compounds, it does not matter what the front of the pack says. The ingredient list is the honest version of the label, and it is always on the back.

Sodium and fat: the two figures most parents overlook

Sodium is the one that surprises most parents when they start looking. Children's recommended daily sodium limits are considerably lower than adults: the WHO suggests under 1000mg per day for children aged 4 to 8, rising to 1500mg for ages 9 to 13. Many popular packaged snacks aimed at children contain 150 to 300mg per serving. For a child eating three or four different packaged products across the day, sodium accumulates quickly and usually without anyone noticing.

Salt makes snacks more palatable and extends shelf life, which is why it appears so consistently in processed children's snacks. A natural dried fruit snack contains virtually none. That gap is worth being aware of when you are building a snack rotation.

Fat tends to worry parents more than it should. The distinction between saturated and unsaturated fat matters significantly more than the total fat figure. Dried fruit and nuts carry some fat, but it is predominantly unsaturated. A wafer made with palm oil carries a higher saturated fat load. When fat appears on a label, check the breakdown rather than reacting to the total.

When a label reads like that, you do not need to run through the checks above. The ingredient list has done the work for you. No added sugar to trace through the table. No E-numbers to look up. No artificial flavour category standing in for an actual ingredient. The distance between a three-ingredient dried fruit product and a twelve-ingredient fruit-flavored confection is the distance between a snack and a sweet. The label tells you which one you are holding.

A six-step checklist for the snack aisle

You do not need to memorise everything above. Here is a short version that takes about thirty seconds once you have done it a few times.

First, find the serving size. Are the figures per serving or per 100g? Adjust all subsequent numbers accordingly.

Second, check the sugar figure for the actual portion your child will eat, not the per-100g number.

Third, read the first three ingredients. Are they recognizable whole foods, or is a sugar or sweetener leading the list?

Fourth, scan for additives. Any azo dyes in the E100-E180 range, sodium benzoate (E211), or vague "artificial flavour" entries?

Fifth, note the sodium. For a child's snack, under 150mg per serving is a reasonable benchmark.

Sixth, test the front-of-pack claims against the back. "Natural," "no nasties," "real fruit." Does the ingredient list support any of it?

Six steps. Thirty seconds. It sounds like a lot the first time and becomes automatic quickly. Most snacks fail somewhere in the first three steps, which makes the decision faster than it looks.

Why the packaging works against you, and what to do about it

The snacks most aggressively marketed to children are often the ones that perform worst against these checks. Bright packaging, cartoon characters, claims like "lunchbox ready" or "school snack approved": all of it is aimed at children and at parents who are making fast decisions in a busy aisle. The nutritional content is designed around shelf life, flavour intensity, and cost, not around what a growing child actually needs from a snack.

Dried mango, nutritionally, outperforms most things in the children's snacks aisle. The problem is that a clear bag of dried mango does not compete visually with a foil tube that has a cartoon on it. Children choose the cartoon. That is a reasonable response to packaging design. But parents are the ones putting things in the trolley.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does "per 100g" mean on a snack label?

Per 100g means the nutrition values apply to 100 grams of the product, not to the whole pack. If your child's snack pouch weighs 35g, the actual calories, sugar, and sodium in the packet are roughly 35 percent of whatever is listed per 100g. Always check serving size first before reading any other number on the panel. Source: FSSAI Food Safety and Standards (Labelling and Display) Regulations, 2020

How do I spot added sugar on a kids' snack label in India?

Check the ingredient list, not just the nutrition panel. Ingredients are listed in descending order by weight under FSSAI labeling rules. If glucose, glucose syrup, sucrose, cane sugar, dextrose, maltose, corn syrup, fructose syrup, or invert sugar appears in the first three ingredients, the product is built primarily on added sweetness. A product made from real fruit will list the fruit itself first. Source: WHO, Sugars Intake for Adults and Children

Are E-numbers on snack labels safe for children?

Not all E-numbers are harmful, but certain ones are worth avoiding for children. The azo dye group including tartrazine (E102), sunset yellow (E110), quinoline yellow (E104), carmoisine (E122), allura red (E129), and ponceau 4R (E124) must carry a behavioral warning in EU-regulated markets. The simplest approach is to choose snacks with short ingredient lists made from real food, which will not contain synthetic dyes or preservatives regardless. Source: European Food Safety Authority, Re-evaluation of six food dyes

Does dried fruit have too much sugar for children?

Real dried fruit without added sugar is not nutritionally equivalent to a product with added sugar, even though both taste sweet. Dried fruit retains the fiber of the original fruit, and that fiber slows the absorption of natural sugars meaningfully. A small portion of raisins or dried mango does not produce the same blood sugar response as an equivalent amount of refined sugar. The concern applies mainly to large portions, or to products where extra sugar has been added to the fruit during processing. Source: NIH, The effect of high fiber snacks on digestive function and diet quality in children

What should the first ingredient be on a healthy kids' snack?

The first ingredient should be a recognizable whole food: a fruit, a grain, a legume, a dairy ingredient, or a nut or seed. If the first ingredient is a sugar, a syrup, a refined flour, or a starch, the product is built primarily on that base regardless of what is printed on the front. For fruit-based snacks, the first ingredient should simply be the fruit, with nothing else added. Source: ICMR-NIN Dietary Guidelines for Indians, 2024
