How to Read Snack Labels

Most packaging is designed to catch your eye on the front. The honest version of the story is always on the back. Here's how to read it in under a minute.

June 2026
8 min read
Fruition Valley

Most parents standing in a supermarket aisle with a child pulling at their sleeve don't have thirty minutes to audit an ingredient list. You flip the pack over, scan it, spot a word you can't pronounce, and either put it back or just put it in the trolley. This guide is for parents who want to move on a little more confidently.

You don't need a nutrition degree. You just need to know which parts of a label actually tell you something useful, and which parts are mostly packaging noise.

Start with the serving size

Before you look at any numbers, find the serving size. It's usually at the top of the nutrition table, and it trips up a lot of parents. A pack that looks like a single portion for a child might say "serves 2" or list values per 100g rather than per pack.

If the figures are per 100g, you need to adjust. A 35g snack pouch contains about a third of whatever is listed per 100g. That sounds obvious once you read it, but most of us skip this step and treat the nutrition table as if it covers the whole thing. It often doesn't.

Get the serving size right first. Everything else depends on it.

Sugar: the number that matters most

There is plenty of debate about sugar, but the practical guidance for children is fairly straightforward. Most paediatric nutrition guidelines, including those from the WHO and NHS, suggest limiting free sugars to under 19g per day for children aged 4 to 6, and 24g for ages 7 to 10. Free sugars means anything added, plus sugars naturally present in honey, syrups, and fruit juices.

On Indian packaging in FSSAI format, you'll see "total carbohydrates" broken into "of which sugars." That second number is what you want. Fruit-based snacks will show sugar here because dried fruit contains natural fructose. That's different from added sugar. The way you tell them apart is by checking the ingredient list, not the nutrition table.

A label that shows 12g of sugar per serving is very different depending on whether that 12g comes from dried mango or from glucose syrup and sucrose. The nutrition table doesn't tell you which. The ingredient list does.

The ingredient list tells you more than the nutrition table

Ingredients are listed in descending order by weight. The first ingredient is the largest by quantity in the product. If sugar, glucose syrup, or any sweetener variant appears in the first three, you're looking at a sweet treat with other things around it, not a snack with a little sugar added.

The reason parents often miss this is that manufacturers use many names for added sugar. A few to know: glucose, dextrose, maltose, corn syrup, fructose syrup, invert sugar, sucrose. These are all the same category under different names. The word "sugar" alone is just one of them.

If the first ingredient is a recognisable whole food (dried mango, oats, strawberries, milk) that's a better sign. It doesn't make the product perfect, but it tells you what the product is mostly made of.

Artificial additives: four categories worth checking

Most parents know to look for "artificial colours" but fewer know which ones to watch for. Here are the four categories that appear most often in children's snacks.

Artificial colours. In the EU, products containing six specific azo dyes must carry a warning that they may have an adverse effect on activity and attention in children. These include tartrazine (E102), sunset yellow (E110), and carmoisine (E122), among others. India's FSSAI has restrictions in this area too, though enforcement is inconsistent. If you see E-numbers in the E100 to E180 range, it's worth a quick check.

Preservatives. Sodium benzoate (E211) is one to know by name. Research has linked it to increased hyperactivity in children when combined with certain colourings. Most dried fruit snacks don't need it at all.

Artificial flavours. "Natural and artificial flavours" is a label phrase that tells you almost nothing specific. Products with genuinely simple ingredients list the actual ingredient, not a flavour category.

Emulsifiers and stabilisers. Less common in simple snacks than in processed dairy or cereal bars. Their presence signals a more processed product overall.

What "natural" on the front of the pack actually means

In most markets, including India, "natural" is not a regulated term on food packaging. There is no legal definition that stops a product calling itself natural while containing added sugars, preservatives, and synthetic colours. It's a marketing description, not a standard.

The only way to check a natural claim is to look at the ingredient list and count how many ingredients you recognise as whole foods. A genuinely natural product reads like a shopping list: dried mango. Or: oats, dates, almonds. Short, readable, nothing you'd need to look up.

If a product has fifteen ingredients including three with E-numbers, it doesn't matter what the front of the pack says. The ingredient list is always the honest version.

Sodium and fat in children's snacks

Sodium is the one most parents overlook. Children's recommended daily sodium limits are well below adult levels. The WHO suggests under 1000mg per day for children aged 4 to 8. Many popular packaged snacks for children contain 150 to 300mg per serving. If your child is eating three or four snacks a day from different packaged products, sodium adds up faster than most parents expect.

Fat tends to worry parents more than it should. The distinction between saturated and unsaturated fat matters more than the total fat number. Dried fruit and nuts carry some fat, but mostly the unsaturated kind. A wafer cooked in palm oil carries saturated fat. Check the breakdown rather than the total.

A six-step checklist for the snack aisle

You don't need to memorise all of the above. Here is a short version you can run through in about thirty seconds when you pick up any packaged snack.

  1. Serving size. Are the figures per serving or per 100g? Adjust before reading anything else.
  2. Sugar. How many grams per actual serving your child eats? Is added sugar anywhere in the ingredient list?
  3. First three ingredients. Are they whole foods or processed ingredients? If sweeteners appear early, the product is mostly sweetener.
  4. Additives. Any azo dyes (E102, E110, E122), sodium benzoate, or vague "artificial flavours" listed?
  5. Sodium. Aim for under 150mg per serving for a child's snack.
  6. Front-of-pack claims. "Natural," "wholesome," "no added nasties." Check the back. The ingredient list either supports the claim or it doesn't.

One thing worth knowing that most parents don't

The snacks most aggressively marketed to children are often the ones with the weakest nutrition. Bright characters on the packaging, claims like "lunchbox ready" or "school snack," cartoon designs, all aimed at children. The formulation inside is usually designed around shelf life and flavour intensity, not nutritional quality.

A plain dried mango, nutritionally, is better than most things in the "kids snacks" aisle. The problem is the packet doesn't compete visually with a foil pouch that has a dinosaur on it. Children choose the dinosaur. That's fair. But parents are the ones buying.
